Everything:
- **Shortwave Numbers Station** — Modeled on real numbers stations (Lincolnshire Poacher, UVB-76). Posts follow strict broadcast protocols: a call sign, a count, then groups of numbers or phonetic alphabet words. The station generates encrypted streams of text formed by using plain english OTPs to encode plain english messages. Cipher challenge a la 1057boy and Cryptok. (Code should be impemented in an obfuscated way.)
- <s>**Celestial Ephemeris** — Post real astronomical ephemeris data (planet positions, moon phase, sunrise/sunset for your latitude) formatted as terse navigational almanac entries. No embellishment, no astrology — just the raw numbers of where things actually are in the sky right now. The channel is a clock that runs on planetary mechanics.
  </s> (already have slack-werewolf-notifications)
- **The Odyssey in Dactylic Drip** — Post 5 lines of the Fagles (or Lattimore, or Fitzgerald) translation every 4 hours. The Odyssey is ~12,110 lines. At 30 lines/day, the full poem takes ~403 days — just over a year. A 5-line chunk is roughly one complete Homeric image or action beat. The year-long duration mirrors the scale of the journey itself. When it finishes, start the Iliad.
- **One Thousand and One Nights** — Post one story per night, literally at night (10pm or 11pm local time). The frame narrative is real: Scheherazade tells a story each night to survive. Some stories take one night, some take dozens. When a story breaks mid-sentence ("But dawn overtook Scheherazade, and she lapsed into silence"), the post cuts off there. The next night picks up. The channel runs for approximately 2.7 years. You experience the text the way the Sultan does.
- **Borges Ficciones, One Paragraph Per Day** — Borges stories are 3-8 pages, ~15-40 paragraphs each. One paragraph per day means each story takes 2-6 weeks. The channel moves through all 17 stories in *Ficciones* over roughly a year and a half. Borges paragraphs are so dense and self-contained that a single paragraph per day is the right unit — each one is a complete thought that rewards rereading. When a story ends, one day of silence, then the next title appears.
- **The Divine Comedy as Descent and Ascent** — Dante's *Commedia* is 14,233 lines in 100 cantos. Post one tercet (3 lines) every hour. At 24 tercets/day, each canto (115-160 lines) takes roughly 2-3 days. The full poem takes ~200 days. The design choice: post frequency changes with the cosmology. In Inferno, tercets post every 45 minutes (urgency, descent, crowding). In Purgatorio, every 60 minutes (patience, steady climbing). In Paradiso, every 90 minutes (expansion, light, eternity slowing down). The pacing is structural. You feel the architecture.
- **First and Last** — Post the first sentence and last sentence of a novel, side by side, with one line on what changed between them. The channel compresses entire narrative arcs into a diptych. "First: 'Call me Ishmael.' Last: 'It was the devious-cruising Rachel, that in her retracing search after her missing children, only found another orphan.' — The book begins with a man naming himself and ends with a ship that was looking for lost children finding him instead. He went looking for meaning and became someone else's accident."

Roman version:
- **Tacitus Annals Drip** — One sentence every 2 hours from the *Annals*. Tacitus writes with surgical contempt — every sentence is a verdict disguised as reportage. At this pace, his descriptions of Tiberius withdrawing to Capri while informers terrorize Rome land one at a time like indictments. The full text takes about 8 months.
- **Signs and Portents** — Posts only the omens, prodigies, and portents extracted from Livy, Dio Cassius, and Suetonius. "A two-headed calf was born in Campania." "Lightning struck the Temple of Jupiter." "The emperor's statue was seen to sweat." No commentary. Just the Roman news feed of a civilization reading its own entrails for confirmation that something has gone wrong.
- **The Proscription Lists** — Every day at dawn, post one name from Sulla's or the Second Triumvirate's proscription lists, with a one-line note on who they were. Property confiscated. Killed by soldiers. Betrayed by a slave. Escaped to Sicily. The channel runs for months. You start recognizing the pattern: first the enemies, then the inconvenient, then the wealthy.
- **Caligula's Daily Briefing** — Curated real quotes and incidents from Caligula's reign (sourced from Suetonius, Dio, Seneca), posted as if they're press releases. "The emperor has appointed Incitatus to the priesthood." "The emperor reminds the Senate of his prerogative: 'Remember, I can do anything to anybody.'" No satire needed. The primary sources are enough.
- **Cicero's Letters, Real-Time** — Post Cicero's actual letters (*Ad Atticum*, *Ad Familiares*) one per day, in chronological order. You watch a brilliant man try to save a republic through procedure while the men with armies ignore him. The tone shifts from confident to anxious to desperate to resigned. ~800 letters. Over two years, you live through the fall.
- **The Debasement Index** — Track the actual silver content of Roman coinage over time, one data point per day, starting from Augustus (97% silver) through the Crisis of the Third Century (2% silver). Just the number and the emperor's name. The channel is a slow-motion chart of institutional collapse measured in metallurgy. When the number drops fast, you know what's happening without being told.
- **Emperors' Last Words** — One post per week. Just the emperor's name, how they died, and their reported last words. Augustus: "Have I played the part well? Then applaud as I exit." Nero: "What an artist dies in me." Vitellius, dragged through the streets: "And yet I was once your emperor." Some have no last words. Just "killed by the Praetorian Guard."
- **Bread and Circuses Schedule** — Posts the Roman festival calendar (*fasti*) in real time, mapped to modern dates. Every few days: another holiday, another games, another distribution. The channel makes visible how much of Roman public life was engineered distraction. On festival days, post what was actually happening politically that week, sourced from historians. The contrast is the content.
- **The Fall, One Entry Per Day** — Gibbon's *Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* is ~3,500 pages. Post one paragraph per day. The channel runs for approximately 6-7 years. Gibbon's thesis — that the collapse was gradual, internally generated, and invisible to those living through it — unfolds at the same pace it describes. You are always in the middle of the decline. You never see the moment it becomes irreversible, because there isn't one.
